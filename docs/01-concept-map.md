# Concept Map

AI Cities maps urban economics concepts onto agentic AI infrastructure. The claim is not that AI systems are literally cities. The claim is that urban economics provides useful primitives for reasoning about shared environments where local actions create system-level consequences.

| Urban economics concept | Original meaning | AI Cities analogue | Repo model/framework | Confidence |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Scale economies | Lower average cost or higher productivity as output increases | Agent/tool specialization and workflow productivity | Agentic Leverage | Strong |
| Agglomeration economies | Productivity gains from clustering people, firms, labor, suppliers, and knowledge | Gains from clustering agents, tools, memory, data, and workflows | Agentic Leverage / AI City Subsystems | Strong |
| Transport costs | Cost of moving people, goods, information, or effort across distance | Tool-routing, handoff, search, dependency traversal, and coordination costs | Congestion / Agentic Leverage | Moderate |
| Centralized versus dispersed production | Tradeoff between concentrated production and distributed activity across locations | Central model/control plane versus distributed local agents/tools | Context Allocation / Risk Zoning | Moderate |
| Retail agglomeration | Benefits from nearby complementary shops and shared customer traffic | Complementary tools, plugins, marketplaces, and workflow clusters | AI City Subsystems | Moderate |
| Urban spatial structure | How centrality, density, land values, and distance shape city form | Centrality, density, and distance from core resources | Context Allocation | Moderate |
| Land scarcity | Scarce land forces allocation decisions and raises the value of central locations | Scarce context, memory, compute, attention, and human review | Context Allocation | Strong |
| Sprawl and land-use controls | Unmanaged expansion and regulatory constraints on where activities belong | Tool sprawl, workflow sprawl, model sprawl, permission boundaries | Risk Zoning | Strong |
| Freeway congestion | One more driver can impose delays on many other drivers | Latency, contention, verification burden, coordination overload | Congestion Externality | Strong |
| Local public goods | Shared goods whose benefits are local and hard to restrict to one actor | Shared evals, docs, benchmarks, standards, open models, safety conventions | Public goods future model / Sources | Moderate |
| Pollution | Private activity imposes cleanup, health, or environmental costs on others | Hallucinations, slop, corrupted memory, misinformation, low-quality outputs | BEM / Cognitive Pollution future model | Strong |
| Crime | Harmful activity that imposes safety, enforcement, and trust costs | Prompt injection, fraud, malicious agents, data poisoning, impersonation | Risk Zoning / VIGIL | Moderate |
| Quality-of-life measurement | Measuring welfare, amenities, safety, access, and lived experience | Trust, usability, reliability, cognitive burden, user confidence | Observability / future city health metrics | Speculative |

## Limits of the analogy

- AI systems are not spatial in the same way cities are.
- Compute, memory, and digital artifacts can scale, copy, compress, or decay differently than physical land and infrastructure.
- The framework is useful only where shared agentic environments create measurable externalities, congestion, risk, or coordination costs.
- The analogy should generate hypotheses and design tools, not replace empirical validation.
